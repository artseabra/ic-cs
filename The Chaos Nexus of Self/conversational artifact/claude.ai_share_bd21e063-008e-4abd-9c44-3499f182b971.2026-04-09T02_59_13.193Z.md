tl;dr: A strange attractor is the long-term “shape” that a dissipative, nonlinear dynamical system settles into—neither a fixed point nor a simple loop, but a fractal set that stretches and folds trajectories forever. It’s “strange” because it has sensitive dependence on initial conditions (positive Lyapunov exponent) and a non-integer (fractal) dimension. Yes, it absolutely makes sense to ask whether new strange attractors remain to be found; there are infinitely many systems and parameter regimes, and new attractors (including “hidden” ones) are still discovered in models and experiments.

From first principles (no hand-waving)
What’s a dynamical system?
A rule that moves a state forward in time.
• Continuous time (ODEs): ẋ = f(x).
• Discrete time (maps): x_{n+1} = F(x_n).
The set of all possible states is the phase space; a solution traces a trajectory through that space.

What’s an attractor?
An attractor A is a set that typical trajectories get drawn toward and then remain on. It’s forward-invariant (once you’re on it, you stay there) and has a basin of attraction (initial conditions that end up on A).

The familiar, non-strange options
• Fixed point (equilibrium): the system comes to rest.
• Limit cycle: a stable loop (periodic behavior).
• Torus: multi-frequency quasiperiodic motion (like two coupled rotations).

What makes it “strange”?
• Sensitive dependence on initial conditions: nearby starting points separate exponentially on average. Formally: at least one positive Lyapunov exponent.
• Fractal geometry: the attractor has non-integer dimension (e.g., correlation or Kaplan–Yorke dimension).
• Mixing + a physical measure: typical orbits sample the attractor statistically (an SRB measure), giving stable long-time averages despite pointwise unpredictability.
• Mechanism: stretching + folding under dissipation. Volumes in phase space contract (negative divergence), but the dynamics stretch along one direction and fold back (Smale horseshoe logic), producing a bounded yet endlessly detailed set.

Why dissipation matters
Hamiltonian (ideal energy-conserving) systems preserve phase-space volume (Liouville’s theorem), so they can be chaotic but don’t have attractors in the same sense. True strange attractors live in dissipative systems: fluids with drag, circuits with resistors, lasers with losses, climate subsystems, etc.

Minimal dimensionality
• Continuous time: chaos requires at least 3D (Poincaré–Bendixson forbids chaos in 2D flows).
• Discrete maps: 2D is enough (e.g., Hénon map).

How strange attractors are born (common routes to chaos)
• Period-doubling cascade (Feigenbaum route): a stable cycle doubles its period repeatedly until motion becomes chaotic.
• Intermittency: long calm stretches punctuated by bursts.
• Quasiperiodicity route: tori break down.
• Homoclinic/heteroclinic tangles: stable and unstable manifolds cross and fold, creating horseshoes.

Diagnosing “strangeness” (practical checks)
• Lyapunov exponents: compute the spectrum; λ_max > 0 indicates chaos.
• Dimensions: correlation/Kaplan–Yorke dimension is non-integer.
• Return maps & Poincaré sections: reveal stretching/folding.
• Power spectrum: broadband (though not white noise).
• Embedding from data (Takens): reconstruct an attractor from a single scalar time series, then test the above on the reconstruction.

Does it make sense to ask if “undiscovered” strange attractors exist?
Yes—very much. “Strange attractor” isn’t a single object; it’s a property different systems can have in different parameter regimes. Because:
• There are infinitely many nonlinear systems (ODEs, maps, PDEs).
• Small parameter changes often birth new attractors (bifurcations), including multiple coexisting ones.
• “Hidden attractors” (whose basins don’t touch equilibria) were only systematically explored in the 2010s; they can be missed by standard searches.
• Even in classic models (fluids, plasmas, lasers, neurons), new chaotic regimes show up with different boundary conditions or forcing.
In short: many are known; many more undoubtedly exist.

Mental model you can trust
Think of a taffy machine. Each time step, the dough slab is stretched, folded, and a bit of air is squeezed out (dissipation). After many cycles, the dough occupies a complicated, filamentary shape. That shape is the attractor; the stretch-and-fold keeps detail alive, the squeezing keeps it bounded, and the exact position of a sesame seed (your initial condition) becomes impossible to predict long-term—yet the dough’s overall shape and statistics are stable.

A concrete feel for sensitivity (one-minute math)
Take the logistic map x_{n+1} = 4 x_n (1 − x_n). Two initial conditions separated by Δ₀ diverge on average like Δ_n ≈ e^{λ n} Δ₀ with λ = ln 2 ≈ 0.693. • Epistemic boundary: chaos is deterministic but practically unpredictable beyond a horizon; the attractor shape and statistics are what you can know.
